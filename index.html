01

Make the homepage do triage.

The main domain should orient the visitor in seconds, then point them to the right place instead of making them decode a long generic personal site.

  • Lead with a simple explanation of what this domain is for.
  • Use motion and layout changes to focus attention without overwhelming the visitor.
  • Track which route people actually choose.
02

Some visitors just want the cleanest overview of your work.

The portfolio route is the quickest path for anyone who wants a broad look at background, work, and the story behind the projects.

  • Route broad-interest visitors directly to the portfolio.
  • Keep this card high-signal and low-friction.
  • Measure how often it wins compared with project-specific routes.
03

Project-specific visitors should land on the project immediately.

Olivia deserves its own route because the best experience for project traffic is a direct handoff, not another layer of explanation.

  • Use the homepage to explain why separate project destinations exist.
  • Let visitors self-select the exact product they came to see.
  • Track project interest route by route.
04

The same logic applies to Tix.

If someone came for Tix, the right homepage move is a fast redirect through a clearly named route and a little bit of context that confirms they are in the right place.

  • Let the route card act like a clear signpost.
  • Keep the homepage focused on triage, not duplication.
  • Use click data to understand how much traffic comes in product-first.
05

WhatTheChef gets the same dedicated path.

The homepage should make it obvious that some visitors are here for a specific thing, and the best UX is to help them go there immediately.

  • Make the routes feel intentional instead of like a random list of links.
  • Give each project a distinct slot in the story.
  • Let the homepage visually confirm the route before the click.
06

When someone wants to reach out, the form should actually do work.

A contact form on the homepage should save the submission into Postgres and immediately alert you by email so no message disappears into static-site limbo.

  • Capture name, what they do, email, message, and timestamp.
  • Store the submission in the database before returning success.
  • Trigger an email notification so you can respond quickly.
